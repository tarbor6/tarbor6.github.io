The brain is covered by three protective layers. What are these layers?
  • The dura mater
  • The arachnoid mater
  • The pia mater

The dura mater covering the brain has two parts. What are these parts?
  • The outer periosteal layer
  • The inner meningeal layer

Under normal conditions, the meningeal layers are closely apposed. The dura mater consists of two layers—the periosteal layer and the meningeal layer—which separate at specific sites to form dural venous sinuses that drain venous blood from the brain. 

Deep to the dura lies the arachnoid mater, which is closely applied to the inner (meningeal) layer of the dura. The arachnoid mater is separated from the pia mater by the subarachnoid space, which contains cerebrospinal fluid as well as major cerebral arteries and veins. 

The pia mater is a delicate membrane that closely adheres to the surface of the brain and spinal cord, following the contours of sulci and gyri.

The sub arachnoid space contains both arterial and venous blood flow. The pia mater is a thin covering enveloping the brain and the spinal cord.

Trauma to the skull can disrupt these spaces, leading to hemorrhage within the epidural, subdural, or subarachnoid compartments.

What are the consequences of bleeding in each of these spaces?

Consquences of bleeding into these spaces include:

  • Epidural hematoma
  • Subdural hematoma
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage

What are is vascular source of the bleeding for each of these spaces?
  • Epidural hemorrhage: Most commonly results from rupture of the middle meningeal artery due to skull fracture, leading to bleeding between the periosteal layer of the dura mater and the inner surface of the cranium.
  • Subdural hemorrhage: Results from tearing of bridging veins as they traverse the subdural space to drain into the superior sagittal sinus, causing blood to accumulate between the meningeal layer of the dura mater and the arachnoid mater.
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage: Most commonly results from rupture of a saccular (berry) aneurysm arising from the cerebral arterial circle (Circle of Willis), allowing arterial blood to enter the subarachnoid space; less commonly, it is caused by rupture of an arteriovenous malformation.
