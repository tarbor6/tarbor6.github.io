Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) circulates within the ventricular system and subarachnoid space surrounding the brain and spinal cord. It is produced by the choroid plexuses in the lateral ventricles.

CSF flows from each ventricle through the Interventricular Foramen (of Monro) into the third ventricle, then through the Cerebral Aqueduct (of Sylvius) to the fourth ventricle.

It then enters the subarachnoid space via the Median Aperture (Foramen of Magendie) or the Lateral Apertures (Foramina of Luschka).

CSF is ultimately reabsorbed into the venous circulation through arachnoid granulations, primarily into the superior sagittal sinus.

What condition results from obstruction of this flow?

Obstruction of cerebrospinal fluid flow leads to hydrocephalus, characterized by dilation of the ventricular system and potentially increased intracranial pressure.

At which anatomical sites is obstruction most likely to occur?

The most common site of obstruction is the cerebral aqueduct. Other possible sites include the interventricular foramen (foramen of Monro), the median and lateral apertures of the fourth ventricle (foramen of Magendie and foramina of Luschka), and the arachnoid granulations when reabsorption is impaired.
