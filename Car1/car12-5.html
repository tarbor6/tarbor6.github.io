Next we will look at the vascular supply:

Rotate and remove the left thorax and the sternum to expose the right thorax.

We will add the following components of the intercostal vascular and nerve supply.

Add the intercostal arteries and their connection to the descending aorta.
Add the azygos vein, the hemi-azygos vein, the accessory hemi-azygos vein,the right superior intercostal vein and the posterior intercostal veins which all drain through the azygos vein.
Add the intercostal nerves.

Notice that Thoracic Nerve 1 sits on top of the first rib. This is a part of what nerve grouping?

The brachial plexus.

Note that this bundle of vein, artery and nerve follow the costal groove which is on the inferior side of the rib. Why is this clinically significant?

Since this bundle sits under the ribs, placing a needle into the chest for a chest tube or a thorocentesis requires that the clinician place the needle on top of the rib to avoid bleeding.
Add the internal thoracic artery and vein. These run under the costal cartilages and run parallel to the sternum.

From where does the internal thoracic artery orginate? To where does the internal thoracic vein empty?

The internal thoracic artery originates bilaterally from the subclavian artery. (Remember rib notching in the case from CPR Week 1.)

The internal thoracic vein empties bilaterally into the brachiocephalic vein.
Add the sternum and costal cartilages..

Rotate the chest to visualize the anterior to posterior relationships.
